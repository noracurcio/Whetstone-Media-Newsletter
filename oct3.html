LETTER FROM THE EDITOR
INTERVIEW WITH LAYLA SCHLACK BY LYRIC LEWIN

As origin foragers at Whetstone Media, we want to honor all origins. To that end, what are your origins?

I'm a Jewish person of Eastern European descent. My dad is from New York, and my mom is from Montreal, so I'm really in the crossfire of the bagel wars, but I grew up in Cambridge, Massachusetts--a place that's been self-consciously diverse as far back as the 1980s.

What do your origins mean to you?

Growing up in Cambridge meant walking to the Japanese market for red bean paste buns and bowls of udon after school, or to the Greek pizza place, and grocery shopping at the hippie food co-op with my family. It meant occasional weekend journeys to other parts of Boston for Cuban or Vietnamese food, or, of course, to Chinatown. My mostly secular Jewish upbringing meant an endless thirst to know and understand how the world works and a drive to stand up for others when I see them being wronged.



What is a dish that meant a lot to you growing up?

Honestly, as a latchkey kid, it didn't get much better than after-school pasta with margarine and grated cheese, or Annie's mac and cheese. But my grandfather's French toast holds a special place in my heart, as do Maine lobsters, steamed, with drawn butter, eaten the same day they were caught.

What is a dish you create to share with others?

I'm an inveterate tinkerer and experimenter, so I never really cook the same thing twice, but I love to make a big, vegetarian cassoulet-type thing, or any sort of dumplings. I also try to one-up myself every year on my husband's birthday cake, which is absolutely something I make to share with others, as I'm not much of a sweets person.

What drew you to working in food media?

It feels like two separate parts of me! I've always written. My dad worked in B2B publishing, in the IT industry, so I knew what the job of an editor looks like, and it seemed like a good way to make a living doing something writing-adjacent. (I was a deeply pragmatic child.) The process of planning and editing and working with designers to make a print magazine that I can hold in my hands has always been enormously satisfying to me, and I really love working with writers, helping get their ideas into the world.

And then, food. All of my memories involve food. I start thinking about lunch immediately after breakfast, and dinner immediately after lunch. I've always sought out new-to-me foods, and I experience the world through olfactory perception. Food's part of who I am. So that was the initial draw, to put this one thing I love with another thing I love. Now, it's becoming much more about highlighting the way food is an analog of the world's big problems,and platforming the people with ideas for how to make things more just.

What drew you to working in food media?

In 2011, I was offered the once-in-a-lifetime opportunity to join the launch team of BBC Good Food in Mumbai, India. I knew nothing about the food at the time (except that I loved eating it). But it didn't take very long for me to fall headlong in love with food writing. Ten years later, I still feel there is so much I don't know about food. But that's what pushes me to keep going!
