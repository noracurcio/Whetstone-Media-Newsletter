LETTER FROM THE EDITOR
LYRIC LEWIN



Whetstone Magazine is dedicated to origin foraging and diversifying the voices that tell these stories. That’s why this week we want to highlight the work of Seed2Shirt, a company that has dedicated itself to sourcing from sustainable sources in Africa and creating an entirely Black-owned and produced supply chain.

Seed2Shirt, founded by Tameka Peoples, is the first Black woman-owned and vertically integrated apparel manufacturing and print-on-demand company in the U.S. Tameka has worked diligently to create an all Black-produced supply chain, and to “provide products and services that empower the African Diaspora people, are planet beneficial, serve a positive purpose in people’s lives, and make the world a better place.” Seed2Shirt products are ethically and sustainably manufactured from cotton and material produced by African and African American cotton farmers, and finished in small batch production centers in the U.S. and in Africa.

On Monday, Seed2Shirt launched a campaign featuring their limited line, “We Represent Us” which features four artists (@nealsniat, @gocosmicninja, @sagestargate, @deevine.love ) and their visual representation of the Black experience.

A statement on the Seed2Shirt Instagram page said, “We have worked hard to collaborate with amazing creatives to highlight the African Diaspora, specifically within the art, textile, fashion, and agriculture industries. This line is liberation and celebration of the resistance against centuries of redlining, exclusion and systematic sabotage towards the Black Diaspora within these industries. Presented by @misstej Print Team @sheprintsit.”



We spoke with Seed2Shirt founder Tameka Peoples about this campaign, along with curator and environmental equity consultant Teju Adisa-Farrar and She Prints It president and CEO Cher’Don Russom-Reynolds.

Tameka, your story is very compelling. Can you tell us why making clothing sustainable is so important for the climate as well as the importance of having ethically sourced materials and production from Africa to the U.S.?

Tameka Peoples: I’mma be honest, I didn’t start this line necessarily because of “fashion’s impact on the environment”…Yes it's true: the impact fashion has on the environment is a long time coming. Industrialization and globalization of the fashion industry, fueled by consumer consumption patterns — it’s no wonder according to most recent reports that the fashion industry is the most pollutive in the word and accounts for 10% of global carbon emissions. For me it was about why…why wasn’t there a Black-owned producer and distributor of “blank” t-shirts...why couldn’t we create a line that we could know the material was from Black-owned sources?

All the way back to the cotton…and while doing all that, could we not have a purpose to heal people and land and build and empower community?

So, in short, why I chose to make our clothing line in a sustainable and ethically sourced manner, was an intentional MUST for me. As the daughter of all those who have come before me and whose shoulders I stand on in this moment, it was my nature to tap into our industrious nature as a people, to create, to connect (Africans throughout the diaspora – especially in the U.S. to Africa), connect to nature, and to dream and act big — rebuild, be resolute in rebuilding our place in production space. We as Black people are the epitome of creation, so it was the connection to our creative source that caused me to create the line in this way…with intention and empowerment at every stage of creation/sourcing to where we produced — Africa. CAUSE you see fashion or that shirt/piece of clothing you put on your body every day, it’s the embodiment of the earth (cotton), the representation of humanity (the creatives’/seamstresses’ hands who made it) and the extension of your expression; with the creation of this line we had the power to harness all of those things.



As the “first Blackwoman owned, vertically integrated apparel manufacturing and print-on-demand company in the U.S.,” can you tell us some of the obstacles you’ve encountered as well as some of the success stories?

Tameka Peoples: Living in the valley of this statement is heavy for me; I was, and I guess still am, a bit oblivious to obstacles. At many turns in this journey there were hurdles: from being given the run-around in the cotton sector and told “that’s impossible, just work with someone who's already doing it” — then they’d provide a connection to a white-owned brand which had all the tools and resources at their disposal, to facing significant monetary barriers to entry that most small Black businesses face. Not to mention the language barrier faced (i.e. French to English, etc.) when working in Africa. I persisted and was resolute in the pursuit of what was setting my soul on fire, it [was] as if all my ancestors were imparting strength in me to continue, not stop.

The successes that followed were telling. We sold out of our first small batch order, we were featured in Fashion Revolution Week USA, garnered support from National Gay Lesbian Chamber of Commerce – Communities of Color Initiative and Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson, we were featured in Usher’s New Look Foundation / UNL Entrepreneurship Crawl, selected by Fibershed to participate in their Regional Fiber Manufacturing Initiative for our regional production vision in California. One success that’s the most profound to me and the team…was signing a 5-year partnership with Burkina Faso Cotton Farmer for our Seed2Shirt Farmer Enrichment Program and being told by the advisor to the President – “You’re home now and we can’t wait to get started.” It was the ultimate confirmation…and we’re just getting started.

What do you all see as a hindrance or misconception that the general U.S. population has about sustainability when it comes to clothing?

Tameka Peoples: The biggest misconception I’ve found has been around the “fast and cheap” approach to fashion. We have to work on re-educating the consumer on the value of clothing. We are paying a price for fast and cheap, and it not only impacts the land but people as a part of the fashion ecosystem. Also, I’ve found that there is a “bit of” an elitist, exclusionary feel to eco fashion brands; very rarely do you see diverse production represented as leaders or trailblazers in this space and at Seed2Shirt we aim to change that.

Teju Adisa-Farrar: The myth that is most dangerous is believing that clothing can be sustainable without transforming the globalized supply chains, which are the infrastructures of inequity. Sustainability is not just about the materials used, it is about being able to provide for future generations without violence or oppressive hierarchies. It’s about livelihood conditions, agency, dignity in work, and acknowledgment of cultural practices and lineages.

Can you talk about how you connected to the four artists who are featured in your newest collection?

Tameka Peoples: Teju,was my connection point to the artists in this campaign, with the exception of the artist Deandra. It was a unique experience working through this campaign in a digital, COVID-19 environment. Even though we all never met in person, I was exposed to each artist's platform and their work; we were able to cross-collaborate on theme, approach, piece description, commissioning, and much more. It was a real pleasure and everyone was such a professional in their space. It was a good experience, we learned a lot, and the artists made beautiful pieces.

Teju Adisa-Farrar: The first artist I invited to the campaign was Jessica Jones. She lived next door to me in West Oakland when we were kids for a few years. My brother and I would climb over our backyard fence into hers to play. She was always so creative. Even though we have not lived in the same city over the years, I’ve always loved her art and the way she shows Black people living life authentically and unbothered.

While I was living in Brussels doing my master’s, I met Neals Niat. My thesis was about how the environment of Black artists and activists in Western cities is shaped in part by their identity. As a Cameroonian-French artist who has lived in Paris and Brussels, Neals understood very well. We became friends, as I was drawn to his art that celebrated Cameroonian culture through a modern lens.

I came across the work of Sydney Cain (aka Sage Stargate) at the now-closed Ashara Ekundayo Gallery, which uplifted the art of Black woman and non-binary creators. Sage’s work was so powerful and subtle, and especially the materials used, such as graphite and chalk. I also identified with their story of being born and bred in the Bay Area and watching the Black population decrease over time due to gentrification.

Tameka brought in Deandra, and I was immediately in love with his art. The design for this campaign especially brings in the ethereal element of space that humbles us all and reminds us to take space in a world that tries to relegate us to the margins.

Teju, you've written about "The Act of Getting Dressed." Can you tell our readers why it's critical to know not only where your food comes from, but also where your clothing originates from?

Teju Adisa-Farrar: The dominant food systems and fiber supply chains that currently exist are born out of an imperative for profit, environmental degradation, and labor exploitation of Black, Brown and Indigenous peoples. Understanding where our food and fiber comes from allows us to recognize that we have to transform our way of producing, eating, and getting dressed in order to maintain our planet’s livability for humans and other species. The overlooked social relations involved in knowing where our food and fiber comes from shed light on the injustices that we need to transform in order to be as sustainable as possible.



Once you had the artists secured, how did you go about the printing process?

Cher’Don Russom-Reynolds: [My company] She Prints It was started to support small, woman-owned businesses with high quality promotional products and print services. The goal was [that] simply, via print and promotional products, we could help women-owned businesses play in the sandbox with the big boys while still featuring their unique appeal.

Since we started in 2017, it has grown to be so much more to the community — but is also a part of my life's mission. I joined this campaign with Seed2Shirt for some of the same reasons I started my company. I believe that money is power and that it can be used to empower others. I want to empower women in business, but also to be a part of demanding a space for women of color in the "wealth room." What this means is that Black people (and Black women in particular) have created, developed, and produced some of the most impactful and influential products and brands in the world — but we have less than 1% of the wealth. I want to be a part of something that empowers Black woman ownership from beginning to end, but also something that will push people who care about the community into positions of wealth so they can then empower others. They always say that it is lonely or empty at the top, well I want that thing up. People like Tameka are doing the people's work and will be successful; I'm just excited to support her in changing the world for the better. Here's more about She Prints It."

Please tell our Whetstone readers about your campaign and how they can support going forward.

Tameka Peoples: I love that Whetstone readers are going to be exposed to Seed2Shirt through this campaign. This campaign is a culmination of great visionaries —Teju Adisa Ferrar and Tameka - Seed2Shirt’s work and [Cher’Don’s] She Prints It – print prowess and the artist’s visual interpretation…colliding and collaborating to bring forth a beautiful opportunity to use our shirts as a canvas and a bridge. A bridge to heal, grow, and create. Most importantly, to learn more about each amazing person involved with the campaign: from the artist, to the fulfillment team, to the farmers and the makers of your shirt. Support by buying yours here.

What’s also so exciting is to know that Seed2shirt is a MOVEMENT. The readers can learn more about our work and the many ways to support our eco-ethical apparel production company. People can shop for their brand, rock their support through our exclusive line of merch, or support by donating to our Seed2Shirt Farmer Enrichment Program supporting our work in U.S/Africa with cotton farmers; learn more about that here. We have something for everyone at: www.Seed2Shirt.com. The company is about being a part of the U.S. apparel manufacturing resurgence; our work is rooted in healing, representation, and equity. Every bit — every purchase, every option — to uplift our work, we appreciate. A percentage of every sale, 100% of our work/efforts goes into building our U.S., regionally focused, globally connected production center. We don’t see competition; we see opportunities to collaborate and grow in the work we do and plan to do.

Teju Adisa-Farrar: We Represent Us pays homage to the Black diaspora’s diverse and spiritual connection to sustainability, ingenuity, fiber, and art. It uplifts an alternative supply chain that centers dignity, regeneration, and an opportunity for us to reclaim cotton. We Represent Us honors the way Black communities have always represented ourselves in the midst of exclusion and lack of meaningful participation in myriad industries such as regenerative farming, fashion, and art — from pre-colonial African practices of using plants as food, medicine, dye and fiber; to our grandmothers repurposing clothing for children and grandchildren. Black people have always been sustainable, out of necessity and ingenuity. We Represent Us is a place to hold and celebrate all these complexities.
