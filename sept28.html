CHEF WHITNEY OTAWKA WRITES ABOUT

HER TIME AT NOMA CAMP:
It's a Vild, MAD World

Warning: Travel times on Google maps assume you already know how to bike like a Dane.

My head start on the estimated travel time of fifteen minutes (more like twenty-five, Google) to get to the MAD Academy headquarters is quickly diminished by my jet-lagged, 14-month-old son’s tearful goodbye, finding my bike-share pickup location, and rusty navigation skills through the city of Copenhagen from memory.

Pedaling as fast as possible, windblown, and about 15 minutes late, my line of vision lands on two men standing in the doorway of my destination. Framed by the emerald green MAD logo that stamps the door to this think tank, Jack Muirhead and Magnus Nilsson patiently await my arrival. Yes, this is the Magnus Nilsson, former Chef of Fäviken and presently the MAD Academy Director and apple orchard owner. My inner line cook of years past slumps in shame. But they greet me kindly, as does the smell of perfectly brewed coffee and the sight of buttery croissants that adorn the tables surrounded with chatting students. A fitting introduction to the week-long adventure ahead.



MAD Academy is one of the many ripe fruits that grew from the ever-expanding, vild mind of René Redzepi, Chef/Owner of Noma. MAD, meaning “food” in Danish, started as a platform and an international gathering point to generate change in the hospitality industry and food systems.

Over the past decade this seed has grown into a biennial symposium, a collection of multimedia publications, VILD Mad (a foraging resource), and MAD Academy. Noted as its most ambitious project to date, MAD Academy wants to change the world by tackling some of the food world’s largest issues, one person at a time. The academy currently offers two courses: Environment & Sustainability (the class I signed up for) and Leadership & Business.

The five-day course is distilled into lectures, field trips, and group activities. Business strategies such as strategic forecasting, stakeholder theory, and communication are a few of the workshops we participate in. A week-long group project titled “Drillenisse” challenges us to turn a successful restaurant into a more sustainable model (to be presented at the week's end to Mr. Nilsson and team… no pressure). But the tasty bits, the real meat of the program, is the real-life examples and conversations that weave themselves throughout the coursework.

Douglas McMaster, Chef/Owner of Silo in London, floored the group with his lessons on creating and running a zero-waste restaurant. Say it out loud. Zero waste. No trash cans are present at Silo. Dairy is delivered in reusable milk cans, the menu is projected on the wall. Zero waste.

Matt Orlando, Chef/ Owner of Amass, and Kim Wejendorp, head of R&D, led us through a tasting of pure imagination. Cooking with byproducts felt less like a class and more like a visit to Willy Wonka’s factory.

Fish bone noodles, lemon skin curd, lemon skin tamari, lemons used until there is not a lemon in sight, ancient recipes revived (I’m looking at you medieval alaja!). Foraging along the windswept fjords in the Danish countryside with Mikkel-Lau Mikkelsen, director of Vild MAD, reveals sloe berries, green hazelnuts, rosehip, and nettles. It’s the search for ingredients, the rediscovery of what the environment provides, that helped inspire the Nordic food movement.

Visiting with Johanne Schimming, farmer and accidental activist, at Hegnsholt Farm gives us a better view of what a full circle the food system can be, and the struggles to make it happen. Copenhagen’s excess bread scraps: lunch for the pigs. Spent hops: lunch for the chickens. This is the dream for many of us who sign up to take part in MAD Academy. These are not the next food trends, but what the real future of food needs to be to co-exist with our environment. This is what normal should look like.
Nourishment of mind and body are of equal importance to the MAD crew. Our week-long lunch menu, prepared by Copenhagen’s culinary giants, unfurled like a “Best of Copenhagen” city guide. Rustic, hearty bowls of chowder brimming with vivid salmon and perfectly plump mussels in a rich, flavorful cream-spiked broth warmed us on a rainy afternoon. Our day foraging at the fjord was completed by what looked like a window into the sea: a sharp, briny broth ladled over pulled crab, mussels, and plush leaves of seaweed. Taco parties by Hija de Sanchez and Amass fried chicken sandwiches were messy delights.

Our time with MAD Academy was adjourned (befittingly) around a table of smørrebrød and schnapps at Restaurant Møntergade with plates of rugbrød, herring, hard boiled eggs, and fried plaice. All classrooms need to be built around a good meal. It was over these shared tables that the day’s content was analyzed and discussed. Where the lessons learned are present on the plate.

There is a pulse to a space like MAD Academy. In five brief but brilliant days you become a part of this living, breathing organism. The future of food stands quietly on a narrow, wind-swept lane along a quiet canal in Copenhagen. MAD Academy has an unmistakable presence. It is a newly planted seed, at its very beginning stages, that will undoubtedly become a culinary behemoth.

Whitney Otawka is a chef & cookbook author. She most recently lived on the remote and undeveloped Cumberland Island in Georgia, where she wrote her first cookbook, The Saltwater Table, an exploration of the flavors of the tropical south.
