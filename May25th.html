WHETSTONE NEWSLETTER
VOLUME 002


LETTER FROM THE EDITOR
LYRIC LEWIN

For this week’s issue of The Whetstone Newsletter, I spoke with two very important Palestinian voices in the food world.

Nader Muaddi, founder and creator of Muaddi Arak, lives in the West Bank with his family. He has shared with Whetstone readers his work creating the ancient alcohol arak on land that is surrounded by Israeli Defense Forces and settlements.

Leyla Moushabeck is the Cookbook Editor for Interlink Publishing and is dedicated to uplifting marginalized voices in the food world. She lives in Brooklyn with her family.

Both Muaddi and Moushabeck shared their stories and experiences as Palestinians who are experiencing this ongoing heartbreak of apartheid in their homeland.



First, some history:
“I think the most important thing is that the Nakba never ended, because forced displacement never stopped. Sheikh Jarrah is not an isolated case, displacement is widespread, and Sheikh Jarrah recently just became a symbol for what is happening all over Palestine. Israel’s first Prime Minister, David Ben Gurion said “the old will die and the young will forget”. We’ve been reliving the Nakba every day since 1948, so we never forgot.” - Nader Muaddi

Around 70 years ago, the Nakba (meaning catastrophe in Arabic) was the forced and violent removal of Palestinian Arabs from British Mandate Palestine during Israel’s creation. After the atrocities committed against the Jewish people during the Holocaust, some Jewish people wanted to return to their ancient homeland. The British handed over The British Mandate of Palestine to the United Nations. The U.N. came up with a partition plan that allocated more than 50 percent of the land to the Jewish state and the remainder to the Arab state. However, this allocation of land was not a reality.

On May 14, 1948, David Ben-Gurion proclaimed the establishment of the State of Israel. U.S. President Harry S. Truman recognized the new nation on the same day 1 even though this land very clearly was outlined as being for Jewish people alone.2

After the partition was passed violence broke out and Palestinians were forced from their homes to become refugees. “Between 750,000 and one million Palestinians were expelled and made refugees by Zionist paramilitaries, and subsequently Israeli forces, during Israel’s creation in 1947-49.”

What we see today is a continuation of that process. Palestinians forced from their homes, land stolen, military checkpoints in place, a different set of identification papers and license plates, restricted access in and out of certain areas and no access to elections if living in the West Bank or Gaza. This is a clear definition of apartheid. The 1998 Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court defined the crime of apartheid as: an intent by one group to dominate the other, systematic oppression by one racial group over another, and one or more inhumane acts. Palestinians have not been allowed to return to their homes and the small areas where they do live, for instance, the West Bank, have been punctured with Israeli settlements. They are not granted the same legal rights and are policed by Israeli military. They now live on a fraction of the land and cannot move about freely and face the constant reality of having their own homes, gardens, farms, lands demolished and stolen.



Q: Thanks for taking time to be with us, Nader. You and your family are currently living in the West Bank. We are seeing reports and clips from the media and social media on what is going on. What are you and your family experiencing on a daily basis?

Nader’s Answer: “We’re just stuck at home and we can’t really step too far away from home at the moment.

In Bethlehem there are daily clashes between frustrated, rock-throwing, Palestinian youngsters and heavily armed Israelis soldiers.

We can’t really go to a calmer area either, because traveling between cities is dangerous, because Israeli forces are systematically committing extrajudicial executions and Israeli settler lynch mobs are attacking Palestinian vehicles, pulling out the occupants and beating them to death.

At the moment we’re glued to the news. We can’t stop watching it, although it kills us to watch it. There aren’t words to express the pain we feel when we see images of our women, youth and children dying in droves.

Other than that, we’re constantly on Whatsapp and FB Messenger checking in on our friends in Gaza, Jerusalem and within the Green Line to make sure they’re ok whenever we hear something.”



Q: Leyla, as the Cookbook Editor at Interlink Publishing, I’ve seen the amazing projects you’ve worked on: from The Immigrant Cookbook, to Baladi and Palestine on a Plate. Tell us why it is so important that people in the U.S. continue to buy from and amplify Palestinian voices.

Leyla’s Answer: “My family—twice made refugees—instilled in me with passion and urgency that culture is the strongest weapon I have to honor my heritage, promote cross-cultural understanding, and combat the marginalization of Palestinian voices, and that this is my inherited struggle. It has been my passion as an editor and my mission at Interlink to elevate underrepresented voices and promote cultural understanding through food. Both because our food stories can enable us to find our shared humanity, but also because they can be a starting point to break down broader systems of imbalance and oppression. As a Palestinian-American, food has offered me a tangible connection with a cultural heritage that has reached me second hand, and to a time and place that is physically inaccessible to me because of the Israeli occupation and the dispossession of my family.

In Palestine, Israel’s decades-long occupation has restricted Palestinian access to the land, sea, and ingredients that make up the Palestinian table. In the U.S., the most visible representatives of Palestinian heritage cuisine are not Palestinian—we mainly see our food labeled as Middle Eastern, Mediterranean and often, Israeli, while our voices are censored and our humanity is denied. Acknowledging our identity, our culture, our rights to dignity and freedom and our connection to the land has become a political act, so our food becomes part of our resistance. The appropriation and erasure of Palestinian culture actively advances the dehumanization of Palestinians, which enables the US-funded Israeli human rights violations to go unchecked. Q: What are other tangible ways that Americans can aid Palestinians? Protest, donations etc? Is posting on social truly helping or does supporting or boycotting help more?

Nader’s Answer: “Protest, but demonstrations alone won’t change anything, we need people around the world to take concrete actions to ensure that their governments are not complicit in the atrocities that we suffer from.

For example, U.S. tax dollars pay for the bombs raining down on Gaza and the bullets that are killing Palestinians throughout Palestine. Israel receives $3.8 billion dollars per year from the U.S. in military aid alone. That’s more than $10 million dollars per day. Wouldn’t that money be better invested in making jobs in the U.S., or subsidizing higher education, or providing free lunches to kids in elementary school, or in prison reform?

Lobby your representatives to stop equipping Israel to carryout war crimes.

But right now, as an easy immediate action, you can text SIGN PTFDZN to 50409. You’ll be able to send an email and a letter automatically to your representative to support H.R.2590. Super easy and effective.

If passed, it would require the Secretary of State to certify to Congress that no U.S. funding has been used by Israel for any activity that involve human rights abuses.

This bill doesn’t stop the billions of U.S. tax dollars going to Israel, but this type of transparency is a good start.
