LETTER FROM THE EDITOR
LYRIC LEWIN


Meher Varma is a New Delhi-based writer. A trained anthropologist and UCLA PhD, Meher covers food and fashion in post-liberalization India. We are delighted to have her join Whetstone Radio Collective with her podcast, Bad Table Manners. You can learn more about her show in this interview as well as her IG Live with founder Stephen Satterfield. Be sure to give it a watch — and follow her on Instagram.



In your interview with Stephen, you mentioned that your podcast title, Bad Table Manners was a riff on the colonial concept of "Good Table Manners." You mentioned that those ideas of "good table manners" were synonymous with eating with cutlery (rather than using one's hands), use of the finger bowl, and eating in courses. You've written about these topics — will you also cover these origins in your podcast? What can the audience expect to learn about these ideas?

Meher: More than excavating the origins of “good” or “bad” table manners, I think of Bad Table Manners as not only the name for a show, but also a mood I wish to create. I want people to speak candidly, and I want my interviewees, particularly the non-famous ones, to enjoy the airtime on my show that they would normally not get in real time around the dinner table.

The concept you brought up in your IG Live interview about making space for the mundane, and slow conversations around food, is fascinating. What do you hope to highlight that often gets looked over?

Meher: Women’s labor in the context of domestic household work, for sure. Cooking and preparing three Indian meals a day (plus teatime, which is a mini meal of its own) is more than a full-time job, without which most Indian households would fall apart. However, this labor is usually invisible, or taken for granted. From the perspective of a food writer, it is also not quite what makes a sellable food story. But, in Bad Table Manners, I’d like to allow the gendered bodies whose work it is to cook, chop, and clean endlessly to talk about what their days are like without trying to cast this narrative as “entertaining” or “good enough” for the audience.

As an anthropologist and a freelance writer, you have woven in stories of resistance, and new narratives around farming and agriculture. What is something that you want to make sure your audience understands about the farming protests in India? And then, about agriculture at large?

Meher: As I said in the IG Live with Stephen, I think our job is not to edit the sophisticated, informed voices of farmers when we are writing about their struggle. It is simply our job to give them the mic and make sure their message spreads. This feeds into your latter question about what I want people to know about agriculture in India at large: this is not a movement about farming alone, it is a resistance against neoliberalism. And the people who can best tell us these stories are often the people whose lives are most unfairly shaped by its policies.

Any other points you want to make sure people know about your podcast?

Meher: The mandate to cover South Asia is both exciting and daunting. While Season 1 will focus on Indian food and the globalization of Indian food, I will cover stories from Pakistan, Nepal, and Sri Lanka in Season 2 and 3.
