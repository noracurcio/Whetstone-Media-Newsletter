THE HERRING LADIES
OF SITKA SOUND
THESE FISH SIGNAL THE RETURN OF SPRING.
FOR THE PEOPLE WHO COLLECT AND PRIZE IT, ROE IS "THE CENTER OF OUR UNIVERSE."
Text and photos by Bethany Goodrich
along with culture bearer Louise Brady, K’asheechtlaa
The Herring Lady is a Tlingít-Kiks.ádi Clan story


“One central part of living in a rainforest is what feels like a very long fall and a very long winter with a lot of rain. We Tlingit people, through centuries of living here, learned how to go through that in a very graceful and wonderful manner,” begins Louise Brady, K’asheechtlaa.

With ravens calling above and salmon leaping on the horizon, Louise described how the eternity of Alaska’s dark winter is broken by the bustle of early spring.

“People would be finally coming outside and preparing nets, and getting ready to harvest the clams, the cockles, the abalone and deer. Of all of the harvesting, the herring are the first to come back to Sitka and that’s what everybody waits for, that’s where all the excitement is.”

She reaches back thousands of years earlier to one spring in particular.

“Everybody is busy getting ready for harvesting and in this case, there was a young Kiks.ádi woman. The Kiks.ádi were the first people here, and this Kiks.ádi woman looked like she was actually being pretty lazy. There was this rock on the beach and at low tide she would be sitting there in the sun while everybody else was out busy working.

“And one day, she fell asleep and the tide came in. She had really long black hair, and when she woke up, she found that the herring had come and laid eggs in her hair.

“And it turns out she was not being lazy at all. She was singing to the herring. She was respecting the herring, she was singing and asking them to come back. They honored her with that respect and they came back."



“That is why the Kiks.ádi women are known as the Kaxátjaa Sháa, flipping ladies, or the Herring Ladies, and that is our original instruction towards the herring—to respect them.”

“Herring may be tiny, but they are the center of our universe,” Louise says.

A Very Brief Introduction to the Last 10,000 Years

Herring season is a brilliant harbinger of spring in Sitka Sound. Friends from afar gather in the company of eagles, whales, and sea lions—all on great migrations to the final epicenter of this seething fish feast. Herring bolster the foundation of life, and with their return, they shake Sitka from the dark grip of winter.

Sun, snow, and hail compete over the little town in an erratic springtime drama we call herring weather. Soon, eruptions of silver skitter across the water surface, followed by the final act of this complex play: the spawn. While herring are enjoyed whole, reduced for oil, and used as bait to catch bigger fish, the herring eggs in particular sit at the center of the Kiks.ádi table.

Gathering these treasured eggs is a complex practice that demands diligence. The Herring Lady whose songs and thick black hair attracted the herring long ago taught the Kiks.ádi people that herring will spawn on introduced substrates (a surface upon which an organism will grow), as well as seaweed and kelp. This knowledge began the practice of anchoring hemlock branches into coves of turquoise milt, the seminal fluid of the fish. Days later, harvesters pull their branches to the surface, hopeful that female fish have glued a thick tapestry of eggs across the hemlock boughs. The act requires precision and a detailed knowledge of timing, tidal conditions, and location.



So, what do the eggs taste like? The texture is crunch, the sound is a squeaky pop, but the taste? Louise struggles to describe the subtleties of the oceanic flavor. Instead, she shares a story about her great niece.

“We always have a big family feed when the herring return — of course, before the pandemic. We keep the pot of water boiling for dipping the fresh eggs in, and 30 family members come in and out. This little 14-month-old can’t even reach the table, and her mother takes the first herring eggs, and puts [them] in her mouth, and the baby starts chewing.

“And pretty soon, she smiles.

“And then, pretty soon, she does this little dance in her chair and she’s smiling there eating herring eggs,” Louise laughs and shimmies. “That’s her first taste of herring eggs, and she did that dance every time she got a bite. And that’s what herring eggs are like for us. You just want to do a dance, it’s so good.”



They can be savored as thick sheets of daaw, eggs layered on broad kelp, or né, eggs like pearls on strands of hair seaweed, or haaw da.aa, roe on hemlock branches. Eggs are often paired with hooligan fish oil (also called eulachon oil) or delicate seal grease.

However enjoyed, herring eggs are rarely eaten without company. Consuming them is an act of togetherness, a conviviality that has glued the Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian peoples of Southeast Alaska to one another for 10,000 years.

“The joy of spring and the joy of herring with our relatives is part of our culture,” Louise says. “When we have our ceremonies in the fall, everybody wants to have herring eggs to serve. It is a way to honor each other and our relationships and it is one of the most significant ways.”

Nearly 90 percent of herring eggs harvested are shared. Between 70,000 and 380,000 pounds of eggs from our small community are delivered across an intricate web of relationships that extend from Utqiagvik to the Philippines. Eggs may be traded for muktuk (whale meat), eulachon, and other foods not readily available in Sitka. The sharing, however, is less about receiving and far more about the nuanced systems of reciprocity and collective caregiving that lock participants to one another, and to the lands and waters that have sustained complex cultures for generations.

“To share this harvest is just this amazing feeling of completeness, an assertion of ‘this is who we are,’” Louise says. “Everybody has their time of difficulty, and I think we all pay attention to that and respond by sharing.”



Honoring the Herring

Harvey Kitka remembers a time when the herring were so thick on Sitka Sound that their flipping backs echoed like a hailstorm. When Wade Martin remembers, he rattles off a list of coves, beaches, and islands where the herring no longer return. Herring season has been critical for thousands of years and yet the joy associated with this special time is increasingly tinged by fear, loss, and worry. Harvesters estimate needing to work ten times harder than they did as youth. The so-called “Amount Needed for Subsistence,” a management level many fear is artificially low in the first place, has not been met since 2014.

Threats to herring vitality first began during the Reduction Era between 1920 and 1950, when over 1 million tons of herring were reduced into oil. Considered overfished as early as the 1930s, herring have never been allowed to rebound to their historic superabundance. This means that the current management regime for the commercial sac-roe fishery, which targets skeins of eggs for Japanese markets but kills the whole fish in the process, is calibrated to an already collapsed baseline. The vast majority of sac-roe herring fisheries across the Pacific have already closed due to scarcity, leaving Sitka as one of the final strongholds for both commercial and traditional harvest.

Through decades of injunctions, lawsuits, proposals, demonstrations (and more), citizens continue to demand changes like caps on the catch quota, emergency closures, and a re-evaluation of the management model. Through these efforts, small changes have been made. For example, protected areas for traditional harvesting have been established and grown.



Many believe it’s not been enough. Each year, the allowable tonnage for commercial harvest only rises, despite the decreased abundance of older fish, growing local concern, an inability to harvest enough eggs for the traditional harvest, changes in predator dynamics, and the impacts of ocean warming and acidification.

Sitka’s tribal sovereign government filed a lawsuit against the State of Alaska, challenging the state’s management of the herring fishery. In April 2020, the tribe prevailed on the first issue in the case when the Superior Court concluded that the Alaska Department of Fish and Game (ADF&G) had failed to follow regulations that require managing the time and location of the commercial fishery in ways that ensure a reasonable opportunity for the traditional harvest.

The tribe awaits the final decision on the second issue which will determine whether ADF&G violated its constitutional duties to manage the fishery for sustained yield by failing to provide the public with important scientific information regarding the fishery.

Beyond the courthouse, tensions continue to play out on podiums before the Alaska Board of Fisheries, the management body that helps dictate state fishing policy.

“Our traditional Tlingit ceremonies go on for 12 hours,” Louise said. In contrast, the Board of Fisheries strictly limits public comment. Revered Tlingit elders have thus tucked expansive knowledge sets that transcend thousands of years into tight 2-minute testimonies for an industry-stacked board that largely dismisses the concerns as nonscientific and emotionally charged.

Watching honored elders forced off the podium mid-breath, their knowledge dismissed, left Louise heartbroken.

“It was such a Western, alien setting to everybody involved,” Louise says. “Some of our elders remember a time when the herring were so thick right on the beach here, you could stick a paddle up in the water and it would stand up on its own. ‘Oh but the herring are so plentiful’ the scientists respond — but according to whom? That really awakened that responsibility, that original instruction of what my relationship as a herring lady is to the herring. I just decided that I would do everything I could to help awaken that in other people.”



Inspired by the movement at Standing Rock, Louise and a group of allies created the Herring Protectors. “We knew that approaching this in a Western manner wasn’t going to work, that we had hit a brick wall,” she says.

The group organized the Yaaw koo.eex’, a traditional Tlingit ceremony injected with contemporary activism, and timed the first event with the Board of Fisheries meeting in Sitka in 2018. Four members of the board attended, but the goals went beyond pulling Western managers into the Kiks.ádi homecourt.

“The koo.eex’ are also about us. Sitka in many ways is the cradle of assimilation for Alaska,” Louise says. Sitka is home to one of the state’s largest boarding schools, and a training college that for decades operated under the mantra, "Kill the Indian, save the man."

“Part of assimilation is that we have a tendency to lose our Indigenous voice within a Western government,” Louise says. “I put a lot of faith in the power of our culture for being able to reach not only the Board of Fisheries but especially our own people to be able to reclaim that Indigenous voice.”

There were no 2-minute testimony cutoffs during the koo.eex’. The second koo.eex’ in 2019 took nearly 12 hours, beginning with a traditional dugout canoe paddle and ending with hours of singing, dancing, sharing stories, foods, and gifts. The ceremonies build compassion, understanding, and meaningful friendships across boundaries—Native and non-Native, young and old. “Everyone’s invited,” Louise says.

“To see and to hear the elders speaking Tlingit and being able to speak as long as they needed to about the importance of herring was really powerful and healing. For all of the people in attendance to see that there are more ways of relating to the world around us than one. To be able to know that this is the place where you get to be Tlingit and where you will be honored for your knowledge, that’s important.”

As soon as it is safe to gather, the Herring Protectors will continue to host herring ceremonies. Currently, the group is organizing for the upcoming Board of Fisheries meetings scheduled for this spring where several proposals relating to the herring fishery will be heard including proposals to expand the commercial fishery and shrink the protected areas for the traditional harvest.

Covid-19 and an Opportunity for Rebirth

In the early weeks of March 2020, when the pandemic first broke through the United States’ safety blanket, the tiny town hunkered down. Food insecurity, often abstracted by the conveniences of globalization, was suddenly visible across grocery store shelves emptied in a panic.

“It was ridiculous. I wanted to make spaghetti and there was no pasta in all of Sitka,” Louise says. Sitka is isolated on Baranof Island — driving to the next town is not an option. “I mean, everything was disappearing, but the herring still came back.”

The sound erupted turquoise in a magnitude unseen in years. For the second year in a row, the returning herring were too small and uneconomical for markets and the commercial fishery opted out. The traditional harvest, however, continued strong.

“It might have looked a little different because of masks and the social distancing, but it didn’t prevent the elders’ joy of harvesting the herring eggs,” Louise says.

In some ways, the pandemic simulated the type of leanness that Louise imagines her ancestors felt after a long winter in Sitka. It simulated scarcity, and so the depth of renewal when the herring returned was magnified, the joy amplified.



“People were worried too, Where are we going to get our food? To know that the herring eggs were here, that was just an amazing relief,” Louise says. While the koo.eex’ and large social gatherings were postponed, people still shared herring eggs with family, friends, loved ones, elders, and those most vulnerable.

While the impacts of the pandemic continue to be felt and concentrated inequitably on those already marginalized, we are reminded that collective care and traditional systems of reciprocity aren’t just kind gestures; they may mean salvation.

Archaeological evidence shows at least a 10,000-year relationship between herring and people of the Pacific. Imagining that collapsing in this lifetime is a devastating pain that activists and caretakers here are unwilling to accept.

“Because to me, when the herring eggs and the herring come in, it’s a rebirth every year. To not have them, we would be missing one of the most important parts of our lives in this place. It would be like not having that rebirth process,” Louise says.

Food is political, it is ancestral, it is a bond, a gift and a responsibility. Ensuring the health of foodways and lifeways is not a passive act, it is one that takes tenacity, courage, care, and vigilance. Covid-19 is crumbling normality and presenting us with an opportunity to execute changes on a massive scale. We are witnessing a rare opportunity for rebirth. Let us engage in that rebirthing process by building a more equitable world, where caretaking of our communities and the lands and waters that nourish us, is as central to our collective universe, as the herring are to the Kiks.ádi.
